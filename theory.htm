Why We Study Theory

 

            The most infuriating aspect of literary theory and criticism is the resistance to it.  In most cases when a student is excited about what they are learning, others tend to entertain discussions with them about the concepts that have captured their interest in order to learn from what they have learned and to assist them in solidifying their understanding of the subject.  In the case of literary theory, I have met with so much resistance to even mentioning it that I was forced to escape into my room for 48 hours, studying theory to wit’s end without the aid of perspectives other than my own.  The challenge to literary theory from my usual social circles is that theory and criticism “spits in the face” of all literary endeavors by trivializing, over-analyzing, and reading far too much into them.  This ultimately led me to ask – does theory and criticism serve any purpose other than those noted above?  Most of the people who are so opposed to literary theory utilize a poetic method of analysis based upon the ideology of expressive realism with occasional jaunts into a psychoanalytic ideology.  Because most of the theory that is studied in academia has been compared to this ideology, I will present my own case for the learning, pursuit of, and creation of theory.

            There is not written or spoken word that is not intended for an audience.  We begin understanding language to perform the function of communicating with others and eventually tend to use it to speak to others ourselves.  Whether the intended reader or listener is labeled as a subject, a homosexual, a college professor, or that special someone, all authors and orators offer their work to the scrutiny of anyone who is witness to their communications.  All readers, internally or otherwise, are critics of the creator’s work.  Anyone comes away from any communication with an impression.  It is often the objective of many writers to refine their writing ability to the point where they can evoke specific reactions and criticisms through their work.  So then, as you read this, you will come away with a idea of what was said, what you thought of it, and maybe even tell the author later through evaluation notes or direct verbal contact what you would have changed about it.  Thus you are a critic whenever you read.  It might also be noted that you would be practicing a hermeneutic method of initial evaluation – that is, taking this text and deriving meaning or concepts from it.  Furthermore, your criticism will be based upon your individual and societal ideologies, or theories.  Therefore, criticism is the application of theory.  Without theory, critics pose no point, have no objectives, and will have little else to say when they mutter, “This essay sucks.”

            This would bring us to the first beneficial reason why theory and criticism is applied.  For the sake of authors, it is good that we have critics to evaluate our work.  Just as students help other students to understand their studies by offering alternate perspectives, so does the critic offer to the author a perspective on their work.  This can be easily demonstrated within the creative literature classes that refine a writer’s abilities.  The guidelines for critical writing were outlined in the syllabus of one poetry workshop with this rule:

 

… on his/her first readings, the author is not allowed to explain the work, defend the work, or answer questions about the work.  This is intended to allow the author to witness the effect of the poem on the audience and gauge the effectiveness of his/her intention without swaying the listeners’ opinions … (Greene, “Workshop Guidelines-LTCR 3-Creative Writing: Poetry-Fall 1999, p.1)

 

This method is based upon the theoretical discourses of the New Critics who criticized literature differently from the expressive realists.  An author’s intent in the expressive realistic theory goes unquestioned – any work was to be an accurate representation of the “real.”  But the new critics developed the idea of the “Intentional Fallacy,” from which the aforementioned rule is a derivative, by asserting that

 

Knowledge of the authors intentions was ‘neither available or desirable (Wimsatt 1970, p. 3) … Further, the pursuit of ‘sincerity’, ‘spontaneity’, ‘authenticity’, or other expressive values associated with the intentionalism had no relation to the more precise and truly critical values of ‘integrity’, ‘unity’, ‘maturity’, and ‘subtlety’ (ibid, p. 9).  The latter were properties not of the author but of the text.  (Cathrine Belsey, Critical Practice (London & New York: Routlage, 1980) p. 15)

 

Therefore, creative writing professors would not have a valuable method of refining a student’s writing had it not been for a more critical analysis of literature than the expressive realists could provide.  This is one way in which the application of theory, criticism, can benefit a writer.

            We would continue now into other fields of human conduct and participation.  The most open field, though probably not the friendliest, is society and politics.  One cannot deny that social interaction is largely based upon some form of communication.  Among all of the ways we communicate, the most likely candidates for the most common are speech and the written word.  “How far are people constructed as subjects by cultural forms and practices which ‘interpolate’ or address them as people with particular desires or values?” (Jonathan Culler, Literary Theory: A Very Short Introduction, (New York, Oxford, 1997) p. 46)  Semiotics, or the study of signs and symbols, was introduced by the linguist Saussure who testified that meaning was constructed outside of reality and instead in thoughts and signs.  His work has been as inspiration to several theorists to develop certain ideas that would eventually lead to a better understanding of meaning and its relation to society and thought.  One so inspired – Haig A Bosmajian, a professor in the Department of Speech Communication at the University of Washington – took Saussure’s theory to imply another meaning.

 

Labels like ‘traitors,’ ‘saboteurs,’ ‘queers,’ and ‘obscene degenerates’ were applied indiscriminately to students who protested the war in Vietnam or denounced injustices in the United States.  Are such people to be listened to?  Consulted?  Argued with?  Obviously not!  One does not listen to, much less talk to, traitors and outlaws, sensualists and queers … Just as our thoughts affect our language, so does our language affect our thoughts and eventually our actions and behavior … Hitler’s ‘Final Solution’ appeared reasonable once the Jews were successfully labeled by the Nazis as sub-humans, as ‘parasites,’ ‘vermin,’ and ‘bacilli’ (Haig A Bosmajian, The Language of Oppression (New York: Alfred A. Knopf, 1974), p. 6-7).

 

Language would then appear to be so powerful in creating meaning that it can cause such great atrocities as the holocaust.  Prof. Bosmajian is not alone in his theories.  However, as he reveals the ways in which language has been used to create negative social change, others propose that language could be an appropriate vehicle for more positive changes as well.

 

A man may take to drink because he feels himself a failure, and then fail all the more completely because he drinks.  It is rather the same thing that is happening to the English Language.  It becomes ugly and inaccurate because our thoughts are foolish, but the slovenliness of our language makes it easier for us to have foolish thoughts … silly expressions have often disappeared, not through any evolutionary process but owing to the conscious action of a minority. (George Orwell, “Politics and the English Language,” in C. Muscatine and M. Griffith, The Borzoi College Reader, 7th ed. (McGraw-Hill, Inc.: L Denton and D. Dunham), p. 185)

 

One of the minorities that Orwell may have been referring to, women, has spoken, at least in par, also of their quest for deliberate social change through language.  In 1971, the president of the National Organization for Women, Wilma Heide, realized a methodology for social change.

 

Instead of passively noting the change [in society], we are changing language patterns to actively affect the changes, a significant part of which is the conceptual tool of thought, our language (Wilma Scott Heide, “Feminism: The sine qua non for a Just Society,” Vital Speeches, 38(1971-72), p. 402).

 

This theory can be used to banish many of the presuppositions and prejudices of our society, which would allow us to live more harmonious and civilized lives.  Comments such as “Don’t mind her, she’s just a teenager” begin to reveal the true meanings of words that go outside of the dictionary definition.  A teenager, in this case, is meant to imply immaturity, fickleness, lack of responsibility, a rebellious nature, and a lack of meritable opinion or thought.  The time when we are teenagers is constructed into a period in everyone’s life during which we have distinct lack of judgment.  It is even used as an insult among adults.  This may be more indicative of our current social problems of teenage suicide, pregnancy, drug abuse, media affected distortions of the body, and the generalization of authorities and police of teenagers as “being up to no good.”

            One of the best examples of a social movement within the literary community is the feminist movement.  It encompasses perhaps some of the finest examples of criticism with a distinct point and effect on out society and the way people think.  In fact, Maria Lugones and Elizabeth Spelman co-wrote an essay that addresses the contest that theory serves no good purpose by creating a set of goals that all theorists should strive for – should they wish to theorize with some intent in mind rather than mere observation.  Within these standards, I’m sure there are a few reasons why literary theory is a useful and fruitful exploit.

 

1.  The theory or account can be helpful if it enables one to see how parts of one’s life fit together … 2.  A useful theory will help locate oneself concretely in the world, rather than add to the mystification of the world one’s located in … 3.  A theory or account … ought to … enable one to think about the extent to which one is responsible or for being in that location … 4.  … a theory that is useful will provide criteria for change and make suggestions for modes of resistance that don’t merely reflect the situation and values of the theorizer … 5.  Theory cannot be useful to anyone interested in resistance and change unless there is reason to believe that knowing what a theory means and believing it to be true have some connection to resistance and change (M. Lugones and E. Spelman, “Have We Got a Theory for You!  Feminist Theory, Cultural Imperialism and the Demand for ‘The Woman’s Voice’”, in M. Pearsall’s Women and Values: Reachings in Recent Feminist Theory, (Belmont, CA: Wadsworth, 1986) p 26-7).

 

These two feminist theorists seem to be as concerned with the merit of theory as those would challenge it are.  It is also notable that I have seen quite a bit of applicable theory being created from the feminist camp.  The use of post-structuralist theory, specifically deconstruction, is notable on the part of Joan W. Scott.  In her deconstruction of the patriarchal language constructs of polarity, the notion that everything has a direct and equal opposite, she effectively shows how justice in a court case concerning equal opportunity could have been accomplished.

 

In the past few years, ‘equality vs. difference’ has been shorthand to characterize conflicting feminist positions and political strategies … it makes no sense for the feminist movement to let its arguments be forced into pre-existing categories and its political disputes to be characterized by a dichotomy we did not invent … In this usage, the opposite of equality is inequality or inequivalence (Joan W. Scott, “Deconstructing Equality-Versus-Difference”, M. Hirsch and E. Keller’s Conflicts in Feminism, (New York & London: Hirsch and Keller, 1988) p. 138-42).

 

Her contribution demonstrates a “theory that will be useful and relevant for political practice” (ibid., p. 134). In this way, feminist theory is relevant and applicable to improving itself and creating better political strategies for effective change.

            There are “theorists” who deny any relevance of theory as it applies to them or especially as it applies to writing.  They claim that criticism as a practice has become self-reflexive and thus loses its worth as tool to teach writers, only retaining its worth as a tool to teach critics to debate with other critics.

 

Critics are no longer concerned with literature but with other critics’ texts, for the critic yearning for attention has displaced the writer and has conceived of himself as the center.  Interestingly in the first part of the [20th] century, at least in England and America, the critic was usually also a writer of poetry, plays, or novels (Barbara Christian, “The Race for Theory”, A JanMohamed and D. Lloyd’s Cultural Critique, (New York: Telos Press, 19??) p. 52).

 

Criticism may also lose some of its social worth as it detaches readers from literature.  Literature, more so than theory, is directly communicated from our society.  It is, in essence, an undertaking of explanation and consideration for the now.  Theory cannot evolve any more than literature can without fresh and pertinent material.  If literature is about out changing world than theory is about the changing world of literature.  But, as I have said before, reading, in essence, is a critical process.  It could be said that if professors and educators had not criticism of opinion of the literary theories presented to them, that they would not be able to teach it.  Christian’s commentary could be thought of as a critical analysis of context or a historical perspective.  She criticizes critics’ criticism of other critics.  But, without being able to discuss or debate theory with one’s peers (as I have been forced to do), one would be inhibiting the critical process that led us to theories that are more applicable now.  They are not applicable at the time they were written much in the same way Trigonometry was not linked to the mathematical representation of waves when Pythagoras discovered it.  And were it not for the applicable theory that came before, we may be worse off.

 

A speaker who uses that kind of phraseology [idiom or familiar language] has gone some distance towards turning himself into a machine, the appropriate noises are coming out of his larynx but his brain is not involved as it would be if he were choosing his words for himself.  If the speech he is making is one that he is accustomed to make over and over again, he may be almost unconscious of what he is saying, as one is when on utters the responses in church.  And this reduced state of consciousness, if not dispensable, is at any rate favourable to political conformity. … This invasion of one’s mind by ready-made phrases (lay the foundation,achieve a radical transformation) can only be prevented if one is constantly on guard against them, and every such phrase anaesthetizes a portion of one’s brain (Orwell, p. 166-7)

 

It is with much gratitude that educators are applying their ideology to theory and then teaching it to others.  Only under the scrutiny of an educated mind will anyone be able to affect change through theory.

            And the study of literary theory is a revelation that should not be denied anyone.  For the more we think about the signs and symbols that make up the alternate reality of language, the more we will understand our writing ability enough to improve, understand our society well enough to change it, and know theory well enough to dispute it.  To those who would challenge theory by asking its merit and worth, you have done theorists and writers a great service.  Your critical questioning of alternatives to the dominant ideology made for a real consideration for thought - consideration that theoretically must be addressed before change is possible.
